Why the team has Claude access

The goal is not to add another tool. The goal is to help the martech team move faster from unclear request to useful first draft, better analysis, and more consistent deliverables.

Better first drafts

Reduce blank-page time

Claude can turn rough notes, campaign briefs, meeting transcripts, or product information into a structured outline, memo, email, or presentation starting point.

Stronger thinking

Pressure-test ideas early

Ask Claude to compare options, identify assumptions, surface risks, and suggest what evidence would make a campaign recommendation stronger.

Reusable assets

Create team patterns

Prompts, templates, campaign checklists, QA rubrics, and research summaries can become repeatable building blocks for the whole team.

Go from easy to advanced

The team does not need to become AI experts on day one. Give them a ladder: use Claude for thinking first, then for reusable outputs, then for agent-style workflows when the work has many moving parts.

Easy

Ask and refine

Use chat to draft, summarize, rewrite, brainstorm, and compare options. Keep the task small and give Claude a clear format.

Repeatable

Create artifacts

Ask Claude to create a reusable guide, checklist, template, table, or simple interactive artifact that the team can keep editing.

Agent-style

Assign helper roles

Ask Claude to act as researcher, strategist, editor, QA reviewer, or analytics translator, then combine the outputs into one recommendation.

Advanced

Use Cowork or Code

Move to Cowork or Claude Code when Claude needs to work across files, inspect a folder, update assets, run checks, or coordinate subagents.

Chat, Cowork, and Code

Choose the surface based on the kind of work. Use regular Claude chat for thinking and content. Use Cowork-style file work when Claude needs to operate across a selected workspace. Use Claude Code for technical tasks that involve repositories, scripts, tests, or implementation.

Chat answers and drafts

When the output is a recommendation, memo, outline, summary, or reusable artifact, start in chat.

Rule of thumb

Rule of thumb

Code changes systems

Agents and team helpers

An “agent” is best explained as a specialized helper with a job, context, tools, and a success check. The team can start with simple role prompts in chat, then later use subagents or agent teams in Claude Code for larger work.

Beginner

Role-based helper

Tell Claude: “Act as a lifecycle marketing strategist” or “Act as a landing page QA reviewer.” This gives Claude a lens for the answer.

Intermediate

Panel of helpers

Ask Claude to answer from multiple roles: strategist, analyst, copywriter, and operations lead. Then ask it to reconcile the best recommendation.

Advanced

Subagents and teams

In Claude Code, subagents can investigate in separate context windows, while agent teams can coordinate larger projects when enabled.

Extreme Ownership: a martech curriculum

A self-paced learning path inspired by the leadership principles popularized in Extreme Ownership by Jocko Willink and Leif Babin. This is not a reproduction of the book. It walks the team through all twelve principles, chapter by chapter, and translates each one into how a healthcare martech team owns outcomes across web, CRM, Salesforce Marketing Cloud, data, reporting, content, and leadership.

Team operating principle

Own the mission. Own the gaps. Own the handoff.

Extreme ownership means the team stops asking “who dropped this?” and starts asking “what did we miss, what will we change, and who owns the next action?”

Start here

How to use this curriculum

Twelve short chapters grouped into three parts. Each chapter gives you the core idea, what it looks like on our team, and one simple exercise you can finish in a few minutes. You do not need the book to follow along.

1

Read the principle. One idea per chapter, in plain language.

2

Map it to your work. See how it shows up in web, CRM, email, data, and reporting.

3

Do the exercise. A 5-minute action that makes the principle real, then capture it in the practice lab at the end.

I
Part One

Winning the war within

Ownership starts with the person in the mirror before it ever reaches the team.

1 Chapter
Own the outcome

Extreme Ownership

The principle

You own everything in your world. When something goes wrong, the first place to look is the mirror, not the people around you. Blame ends progress; ownership starts it.

On our team

If a launch misses, the question is not “who dropped this?” It is “what did I fail to clarify, verify, or communicate — and what will I change?”

Try it 5 min

Take one recent miss. Write a single sentence that begins with “I own…” and names something you could actually have controlled.

2 Chapter
Set the standard

No Bad Teams, Only Bad Leaders

The principle

There are no bad teams, only bad leaders. The standard you walk past is the standard you accept. Performance follows the bar that has been set and held.

On our team

When a handoff keeps breaking, fix the standard and the system around it — a checklist, a definition of done — instead of quietly blaming one person.

Try it 5 min

Name one recurring quality gap. Write the new standard in one line that every teammate could repeat from memory.

3 Chapter
Believe the mission

Believe

The principle

To lead others, you have to believe in the mission. If you do not understand the “why,” ask up the chain until you can explain it convincingly to your own team.

On our team

Before executing a priority, be able to state why it matters to the patient or consumer and to the business — not just that leadership asked for it.

Try it 5 min

Pick a current project. Write its “why” in two sentences a teammate could repeat back without notes.

4 Chapter
Check the ego

Check the Ego

The principle

Ego clouds judgment and blocks growth. Confidence is essential, but when ego runs the show we stop listening, hide mistakes, and defend turf instead of solving problems.

On our team

Let the best answer win, whoever it comes from. Data, user experience, and team learning matter more than being right in the room.

Try it 5 min

Name one belief about your work you would be willing to test this month, and write how you will test it.

II
Part Two

Laws of combat

Four habits that let a cross-functional team execute together under pressure.

5 Chapter
Cover and move

Cover and Move

The principle

Cover and Move means teamwork. Every group depends on the others; you support the team next to you so the whole mission advances, not just your slice of it.

On our team

Web, CRM, Marketing Cloud, data, content, PRM, and leadership are one team. Optimize the whole journey rather than perfecting a single silo.

Try it 5 min

Name the team directly downstream of you. Identify one thing that would make their work easier, and commit to it this week.

6 Chapter
Keep it simple

Simple

The principle

Simple plans win. Complexity compounds problems, and complicated plans fall apart when things go wrong. Communicate so the most junior person can understand and act.

On our team

Write briefs and handoffs that anyone on the team can follow without a meeting or a decoder ring.

Try it 5 min

Take one process doc and cut it until a brand-new hire could execute it. Note what you removed.

7 Chapter
Prioritize and execute

Prioritize and Execute

The principle

When everything feels urgent: relax, look around, make a call. Determine the highest priority, execute it, then move to the next. Trying to do all of it at once does none of it well.

On our team

When the queue is on fire, name the single top constraint, solve it, communicate the move, then repeat. Sequence beats scramble.

Try it 5 min

Take today’s task list. Circle the one item that unblocks the most other work, and do it first.

8 Chapter
Decentralized command

Decentralized Command

The principle

No leader can manage every detail. Push decisions to the people closest to the work — inside a clear intent, known boundaries, and a shared understanding of the goal.

On our team

Give owners commander’s intent and guardrails so they can decide and move without waiting for a meeting or an approval chain.

Try it 5 min

Pick one decision you currently approve yourself. Write the guardrail that would let someone else make it confidently.

III
Part Three

Sustaining victory

How ownership holds up over time: planning, communication, decisiveness, and discipline.

9 Chapter
Plan

Plan

The principle

Good planning lowers risk. Define the mission, name what could go wrong, build in contingencies, and brief the plan so everyone understands their role and the standard.

On our team

Every launch plan should name owners, the top risks, a clear definition of “done,” and a rollback path if something breaks.

Try it 5 min

For your next launch, write the top three risks and one contingency for each.

10 Chapter
Leading up and down

Leading Up and Down the Chain of Command

The principle

Leadership flows both ways. Down the chain: give your team the “why” and the support. Up the chain: give leaders the information and recommendations they need — do not expect them to read your mind.

On our team

If you need a decision or a resource, bring the context and a recommendation, not just a problem dropped on someone’s desk.

Try it 5 min

Draft a three-line “leading up” note for one current blocker: situation, options, and your recommendation.

11 Chapter
Decisiveness amid uncertainty

Decisiveness Amid Uncertainty

The principle

There is rarely perfect information. Waiting for certainty is itself a decision — usually the wrong one. Make the best call you can with what you have, then adapt as you learn.

On our team

Ship the reversible decision quickly; save the long deliberation for the genuine one-way doors.

Try it 5 min

Name one decision you have been delaying. Is it reversible? If yes, make the call today.

12 Chapter
Discipline equals freedom

Discipline Equals Freedom

The principle

Discipline — consistent standards, routines, and self-control — is what creates freedom. Disciplined habits like checklists, reviews, and documentation buy speed and flexibility later.

On our team

Standing QA checklists and honest after-action reviews are exactly what free the team to move fast without breaking things.

Try it 5 min

Choose one habit — a checklist or a weekly review — to run for 30 days. Define when you will do it and how.

What should I ask Claude?

Pick the task and copy the recommended starting move. This helps new users choose a productive prompt instead of typing a vague request.

Recommended prompt move

Create a presentation outline

Give Claude the audience, goal, raw notes, and desired number of slides. Ask for slide titles, takeaway headlines, visuals, and missing questions.

Special words that power up Claude

These phrases tell Claude what kind of work to do: ask, structure, critique, verify, create, delegate, or preserve context. Teach the team to use them intentionally.

Ask first

“Ask clarifying questions first.”

Use when the request is incomplete and you want Claude to identify gaps before drafting.

Shape

“Return this as a table.”

Use for comparisons, content calendars, test plans, persona maps, and launch checklists.

Critique

“Pressure-test this recommendation.”

Use when you want risks, missing evidence, counterarguments, and stronger decision logic.

Reusable

“Turn this into a template.”

Use after a good output so the team can repeat the workflow with new campaign inputs.

Artifact

“Create an artifact.”

Use when you want a standalone document, single-page guide, simple app, visualization, or content you will keep editing.

Citations

“Cite sources and flag uncertainty.”

Use for research tasks, especially when web search or uploaded sources are part of the workflow.

Interview

“Interview me first.”

Use when the goal is fuzzy. Claude can ask better intake questions before creating the output.

Evidence

“Show your reasoning as assumptions and evidence.”

Use for recommendations. Ask Claude to separate facts, assumptions, and judgment calls.

Verify

“Give yourself a check you can run.”

Use in Code or Cowork-style work. Ask Claude how it will prove the task is done.

Delegate

“Use subagents to investigate.”

Use in Claude Code when research would clutter the main conversation or requires separate expert lenses.

Plan mode

“Explore first, then make a plan.”

Use before technical or multi-file work. Claude should understand the system before changing it.

Context reset

“Start fresh with this summary.”

Use when a thread has drifted. Give Claude the clean goal, decisions, constraints, and next step.

Best practices for the team

The quality of Claude’s work depends on the quality of the task definition, context, and feedback loop. These habits help the team get better results with fewer corrections.

Context

Give the real situation

Include audience, business goal, channel, timeline, constraints, source material, and what “good” looks like.

Format

Specify the output

Ask for a table, memo, slide outline, checklist, JSON, artifact, email, QA rubric, or executive summary.

Iterate

Treat the first answer as draft one

Ask Claude to shorten, sharpen, adapt to a persona, add missing risks, or create a second version with a different angle.

Verify

Ask for evidence

For research, ask for citations and uncertainty. For Code, ask for tests, screenshots, or command output. For Cowork, ask what files were reviewed.

Protect context

Split big work

Use one thread for research, one for drafting, and one for final polishing. In Code, use subagents for investigation.

Human review

Keep judgment with the team

Claude can accelerate the work, but the team owns accuracy, brand fit, legal review, stakeholder alignment, and final decisions.

Team guardrails

The team should use Claude as a collaborator, not an autopilot. These guardrails give people confidence about what to try, what to verify, when to escalate, and what not to share.

Do
  • Use Claude for drafts, structure, synthesis, alternatives, and QA checks.
  • Include audience, goal, context, constraints, and format in the prompt.
  • Verify facts, data, claims, and source quality before sharing externally.
  • Save useful prompts and examples so the team builds a shared library.
Avoid
  • Do not paste confidential, regulated, customer, or sensitive data unless your company policy allows it.
  • Do not publish Claude output without human review and brand/legal checks.
  • Do not ask for “make it better” without saying better for whom and why.
  • Do not treat a polished answer as a verified answer.
Data

Protect sensitive information

  • Use anonymized or synthetic examples when practicing.
  • Remove customer names, emails, account IDs, contract details, and personal data unless policy explicitly allows it.
  • Do not paste secrets, API keys, credentials, private URLs, unpublished financials, or restricted partner data.
Accuracy

Verify before sharing

  • Check facts, dates, benchmarks, pricing, claims, and source quality.
  • Ask Claude to separate facts, assumptions, and recommendations.
  • For external content, require human brand, legal, and stakeholder review.
Brand

Keep voice and claims aligned

  • Provide approved messaging, audience, tone, and forbidden claims.
  • Ask Claude to flag claims that need evidence or approval.
  • Do not let Claude invent customer quotes, performance metrics, or product capabilities.
Research

Demand traceability

  • Ask for sources, caveats, and confidence level.
  • Do not mix sourced facts with Claude’s guesses.
  • Use research summaries as decision support, not final truth.
Automation

Keep humans in the loop

  • Review outputs before sending, publishing, uploading, or changing systems.
  • For Cowork and Code, ask Claude to show what files it read or changed.
  • Use approval checkpoints before any action with business impact.
Technical work

Plan, test, and preserve

  • Ask Claude Code to explore first and wait for approval before edits.
  • Require checks: tests, lint, screenshots, tracking validation, or sample output.
  • Do not allow broad destructive actions without clear scope and backup.
Collaboration

Make learning visible

  • Share useful prompts, failed prompts, and before/after examples.
  • Create a common prompt library for repeatable martech workflows.
  • Capture unresolved questions in the Q&A board and answer them after the session.
Escalation

Know when to stop

  • Escalate if the request involves legal, security, procurement, regulated data, or customer-impacting decisions.
  • Pause if Claude contradicts known facts or cannot cite sources.
  • Ask an owner to approve before changing templates, tracking, automations, or live content.
Red flags

Watch for risky patterns

  • “Just send it as-is.”
  • “Use this customer data; it is probably fine.”
  • “Make up a benchmark.”
  • “Change the live workflow without telling anyone.”
